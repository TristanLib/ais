## Target Decision

Target journal:

> **The Journal of Navigation** (Cambridge University Press, published on
> behalf of The Royal Institute of Navigation)

Recommended article type:

> **Research Article**

Recommended working title:

> **A Reproducible AIS Trajectory Prediction Benchmark for Navigation
> Risk-Warning Support**

Core positioning:

> This is a navigation-safety and decision-support paper. It should not be
> framed as a deep-learning architecture paper. The contribution is an auditable
> real-AIS evidence chain that links short-horizon trajectory prediction,
> generalization testing, and CPA/TCPA risk-warning evaluation.

## Cost and Access Strategy

Recommended default:

> Submit as a regular Research Article and do not choose paid Gold Open Access
> unless an institution, funder, or Cambridge open-access agreement covers the
> charge.

Current cost notes from the Cambridge author pages:

- Gold Open Access is optional for this hybrid journal.
- The listed APC is GBP 2610 or USD 3655 when Gold Open Access is selected and
  not covered by an agreement.
- For non-Gold-OA publication, the Gold OA APC is not applicable.
- Online colour figures are free.
- Printed colour figures cost GBP 200 or USD 320 per figure, capped at GBP 1000
  or USD 1600 per article.

Recommended decisions for this manuscript:

1. Choose regular publication during submission unless APC funding is already
   confirmed.
2. Keep all figures readable in grayscale and do not request printed colour.
3. Do not pay for optional language editing until after the authorial
   polish/de-template pass.
4. Use a code/data DOI to improve reproducibility credibility without paying an
   APC.
5. Re-check the Cambridge fee page immediately before final submission because
   APCs and agreements can change.

## Official Submission Constraints to Respect

Based on Cambridge's current author instructions and submission pages:

- Submission system: ScholarOne Manuscripts via the Cambridge journal page.
- Cambridge Core account is separate from the submission-system account.
- Article types include Research Articles, Review Articles, Letters to the
  Editor, Forum, Editorials, and Opinion Paper.
- The journal uses single-blind peer review.
- Average paper length is 6,000-8,000 words.
- Average submission page length can be up to 20 pages including figures and
  tables.
- Format target: single column, 12 point Times New Roman, 2.54 cm margins.
- Word or LaTeX is accepted for initial submission.
- If using Word, upload the `.doc`/Word file and the system generates a review
  PDF.
- If using LaTeX, generate and upload the PDF; use the journal's `nav.cls`
  style/supporting files.
- Figures and tables should initially be embedded in the manuscript so reviewers
  see them in context.
- Keywords should not be typed into the manuscript; authors select up to four
  keywords in the online submission system.
- References are not numbered; the end list should be alphabetical by first
  author's family name and follow Journal style.
- Real-world experimentation is favoured; simulation-only papers are judged less
  favourably when real-world evidence is appropriate.
- A competing-interest declaration must be included in the main manuscript.
- AI-tool use must be declared when AI generated text/images or analysed/extracted
  insights from data. Minimal spelling or formatting support does not require
  declaration, but authors remain accountable.
- ORCID is encouraged during submission.
- Supplementary files are allowed; individual supplementary files should be no
  more than 10 MB.

## Manuscript Optimization Plan

### Phase 1: Reframe the Paper for Navigation Science

Goal:

Make the paper immediately legible to navigation researchers, not just machine
learning readers.

Actions:

1. Rewrite the title, abstract, and introduction around navigation risk warning:
   - AIS trajectory prediction as a support layer for maritime situational
     awareness.
   - CPA/TCPA warning stability as the operational link.
   - Reproducibility and strong kinematic baselines as the methodological gap.
2. Replace "deep learning fails" framing with:
   - "simple motion baselines remain strong and must be reported";
   - "architecture-superiority claims require auditable evidence";
   - "prediction evaluation should include downstream navigation-warning
     consequences."
3. State three research questions:
   - RQ1: How do simple kinematic, statistical, and neural models compare under
     an audited real-AIS short-horizon protocol?
   - RQ2: Do model rankings remain stable under temporal and vessel-disjoint
     holdouts?
   - RQ3: How do prediction errors affect CPA/TCPA risk-warning precision,
     recall, false alarms, and missed warnings?

Acceptance:

- Abstract does not claim autonomous collision avoidance.
- Introduction explains what navigation readers gain from the paper.
- Contribution list is about navigation decision support and reproducible
  evaluation, not neural model novelty.

### Phase 2: Strengthen Related Work

Goal:

Build a JON-ready literature frame.

Sections to add:

1. AIS-based vessel trajectory prediction.
2. Kinematic and statistical baselines in short-horizon prediction.
3. Neural sequence models for maritime trajectories.
4. CPA/TCPA and collision-risk assessment.
5. Reproducible evaluation, dataset protocols, and generalization in safety
   critical trajectory prediction.

Deliverable:

- 30-45 high-quality references, with a balance of navigation/maritime,
  transportation prediction, and reproducibility/evaluation methodology.

Acceptance:

- Every baseline and metric is motivated by literature.
- The paper explains why a strong simple baseline is a contribution rather than
  a negative result.
- References are in JON alphabetical author-date style.

### Phase 3: Harden Experimental Evidence

Goal:

Make the current evidence defensible under JON's preference for real-world
experimentation.

Already available:

- Four NOAA AIS source dates.
- Temporal and vessel-disjoint splits.
- Real AIS-derived risk-warning scenarios.
- Non-debug benchmark results.
- Neural proxy-tuning records.

Recommended additions before submission:

1. Add full-day or extra time-block sensitivity run if compute time permits.
2. Add confidence intervals to the main result table, not only in JSON.
3. Add at least one encounter case study figure from `outputs/final_risk/`.
4. Add horizon-wise degradation figure from

5. Add scenario-slice figure for speed/turn/region group errors from

Acceptance:

- Main text includes 3-5 figures:
  - Figure 1: pipeline/data protocol diagram.
  - Figure 2: temporal and vessel-disjoint ADE/FDE comparison.
  - Figure 3: error-vs-horizon curves.
  - Figure 4: CPA/TCPA risk-warning precision/recall/false-alarm comparison.
  - Figure 5: AIS-derived encounter case study.
- Results section reports the key numbers from generated artifacts only.
- Limitations explicitly identify historical AIS, time-block sampling, no live
  AIS stream, and no closed-loop avoidance.

## Claim Boundary for JON

Allowed claims:

- Real historical AIS data support the evaluation.
- Simple kinematic baselines remain strong under the current audited protocol.
- Kalman-style CV is the best current model by ADE on temporal and
  vessel-disjoint holdouts.
- Risk-warning metrics show how trajectory-prediction differences affect
  CPA/TCPA warning precision, recall, false alarms, and missed warnings.
- The project provides a reproducible evidence chain for navigation prediction
  and decision-support evaluation.

Disallowed claims unless new evidence is added:

- The system is a validated autonomous collision-avoidance system.
- The method works on live AIS streams.
- Neural models are generally inferior or superior in all maritime settings.
- The results prove all-day or seasonal generalization.
- The method satisfies operational certification or COLREGs compliance in
  closed-loop navigation.
